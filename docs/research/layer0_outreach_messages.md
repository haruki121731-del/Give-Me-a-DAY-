# Layer 0 — Outreach Messages

**Purpose**: Ready-to-use outreach copy for Haruki to request Mom Test interviews.
**Date**: 2026-03-26
**Related**: `layer0_target_profile.md`, `mom_test_run_plan.md`

---

## Tone Guidance

- You are asking to learn from their experience. You are not pitching.
- Be specific about why you chose them (shows you did homework, not mass outreach).
- Be honest about what you're doing: researching a problem space.
- Be short. Respect their time. Ask for 20 minutes, not 60.
- Do not mention the product by name. Do not describe features. Do not link to anything.
- Write like a human, not like a startup founder running a playbook.

---

## What NOT to Say

| Avoid | Why |
|-------|-----|
| "I'm building an AI tool that..." | Shifts conversation from their pain to your solution |
| "Would you use a product that..." | Hypothetical — produces polite lies, not learning |
| "I'd love to get your feedback on our product" | Frames it as product feedback, not problem exploration |
| "We're disrupting..." / "We're revolutionizing..." | Startup cliche. Kills credibility with quant practitioners. |
| "Quick survey" / "Brief questionnaire" | Signals low-value interaction. People don't want to fill out forms. |
| "As an early adopter, you'd get..." | Incentive framing. Contaminates the conversation. |

## Variant A: Warm Contact

Use when: Haruki knows the person directly or has interacted with them before.

### Short version (DM / text)

> Hey [Name] — I'm researching how people who build systematic strategies decide whether a new direction is worth pursuing before committing serious time to it. You came to mind because [specific reason — e.g., "you mentioned dropping that mean-reversion approach last year"]. Would you be up for a 20-min call sometime this week? Just want to hear how you think about it. No pitch, no product demo — just learning.

### Normal version (email)

> Subject: Quick question about your strategy research process
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I'm spending some time researching how systematic traders evaluate new strategy directions — specifically, how you decide whether something is worth pursuing before committing to full backtesting and implementation.
>
> I reached out to you because [specific reason — e.g., "I remember you talking about the time you spent on that pairs trading approach before realizing the cointegration was spurious"]. That kind of experience is exactly what I'm trying to understand better.
>
> Would you have 20 minutes for a call sometime this week or next? I'm not selling anything or demoing a product — I just want to hear how you think about the early evaluation stage.
>
> Happy to work around your schedule.
>
> Haruki

## Variant C: Cold but Credible

Use when: No existing connection. Reaching out based on public activity/posts.

### Short version (DM)

> Hi [Name] — I saw your [specific post/comment/repo]. I'm researching how systematic traders evaluate strategy directions before committing to full development. Specifically interested in the "should I keep going or abandon this?" decision. Would you have 20 min to share your experience? Not selling anything.

### Normal version (email / LinkedIn)

> Subject: Research question — systematic strategy evaluation
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I found your [specific public work — be precise: post title, repo name, comment thread]. I'm doing research on a specific problem in systematic strategy development: the early evaluation stage, before you've committed to full backtesting and implementation.
>
> Most people I've talked to have a story about spending weeks or months on a direction that turned out to be fragile in ways they could have caught earlier. I'm trying to understand that pattern better — what the evaluation process actually looks like, what makes it hard, and what people wish were different.
>
> If you have 20 minutes sometime in the next two weeks, I'd appreciate hearing about your experience. This is research, not a sales call — I won't pitch you anything.
>
> Either way, appreciated the [specific thing you saw from them].
>
> Haruki

## Follow-Up Message

Use 5–7 days after initial outreach if no response. Send once only — do not follow up more than once.

> Hi [Name] — just bumping this in case it got buried. Totally understand if you're busy or not interested. The offer stands if timing works better later — I'm trying to talk to 3–5 people who build systematic strategies about how they evaluate new directions. 20 min, no pitch. Either way, no pressure.

## Personalization Checklist

Before sending any message, verify:

- [ ] You have a specific reason for contacting this person (not "you seem like a quant trader")
- [ ] You referenced something specific they did or said (post, repo, comment, conversation)
- [ ] You did not mention the product name, features, or what you're building
- [ ] You asked for 20 minutes, not more
- [ ] You said "no pitch" or "not selling anything" explicitly
- [ ] The message sounds like it was written by a person, not generated by a template
